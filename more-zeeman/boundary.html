Hackenberg, A. J. (2010). Students' reasoning with reversible multiplicative relationships. Cognition and Instruction, 28(4), 383-432. Also: Hackenberg (2007), JMB, 26(1), 27-47. Three levels of units coordination: (1) the unit fraction (e.g., 1/11), (2) the composite unit (e.g., 12/11 as twelve iterations of the unit fraction), (3) the referent whole (e.g., 11/11 that defines the size of the unit fraction). Hackenberg's key finding: construction of improper fractions requires having interiorized three levels of units — splitting alone is not sufficient.
Steffe, L. P., & Olive, J. (2010). Children's Fractional Knowledge. Springer. The reorganization hypothesis: children construct fractional knowledge through reorganization of their existing number sequences, not by learning a new kind of number from scratch. Olive (1999) documents how the explicitly nested number sequence (ENS) gets reorganized into a connected number sequence (CNS) when applied to continuous quantities. The CNS is the bridge between counting and fractions.
Vamvakoussi, X., & Vosniadou, S. (2004). Understanding the structure of the set of rational numbers: A conceptual change approach. Learning and Instruction, 14(5), 453-467. The framework theory approach describes fraction learning as a "slow, gradual process" constrained by the presupposition of discreteness. Children generate synthetic concepts — intermediate states bridging whole-number and rational-number perspectives. These misconceptions are not failures but visible traces of the reorganization process.

Negarestani, R. (2018). Intelligence and Spirit. Urbanomic/Sequence Press. The Chomsky hierarchy is not just a classification of formal languages — it is a ladder of computational capacities. The continuity argument (Steffe/Olive, Siegler, Boyce & Norton): three converging lines of research support that fractions emerge from whole-number crisis through reorganization, not categorical leap. Boyce & Norton (2016, JMB) confirm that the number of levels of units coordination students achieve in whole-number contexts predicts their fraction scheme construction. The transition is real but continuous: the learner's counting scheme cannot partition the unit, and it needs to reorganize so it can.
The "automaton IS the denominator" framing: a counting PDA parameterized by base-7 would treat 1/7 as its first tick, 3/7 as three ticks. The automaton itself is the denominator, understood as a recollected process. Common denominators then require synthesizing a meta-automaton that subsumes both base-7 and base-5 counting — the LCM as dialectical synthesis. Whether this is tractable as FSM synthesis or requires genuinely recursive strategy composition is the open question (FRACTION_CRISIS_ASSESSMENT.md, Gap 3).

Three open pieces, each more specific than the earlier framing:

Variable base, at the synthesis layer. The counting PDA is hardcoded for base-10, and a fraction action like the area model carries denominator information explicitly in its trace rather than in the transition table. The open question is whether base can become a parameter that flat-FSM synthesis can vary — or whether varying it requires the recursive composition that flat synthesis cannot do.

ORR-cycle wiring for fraction outcomes. The meta-interpreter does not yet route fraction outcomes through the crisis cycle as recollection-compatible values. This is not a representational limit on the action automata; it is a missing piece of the Observe–React–Reorganize loop. It is a concrete piece of work.

Metamorphic accommodation. The harder open problem (Norton/Steffe): a strategy that calls other strategies that modify themselves, the way a child reorganises a counting scheme into a partitioning scheme. Flat-FSM synthesis cannot do this. Whether the Meaning-Use Relations layer (Brandom, Between Saying and Doing) plus the commitment tracker provide enough scaffolding for a different kind of synthesis is an open research question, not a settled limit.
