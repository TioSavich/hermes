The Bridge

A machine starts knowing only how to count. One tally mark at a time. You give it an arithmetic problem, and it tries. When the counting breaks down, it enters crisis — and must learn a new strategy or fail.

This page connects the Prolog formalization to the catastrophe geometry of The More Machine. Each computation runs against a live Prolog server. The tension it accumulates drives a Zeeman catastrophe machine. When the learner snaps, you see it.

Everything you have felt so far — the snaps, the tension, the nested machines — that is what the Prolog formalization does. This page connects the two: it takes the formal system's internal state (how many inferences, whether crisis has fired, how much tension has built) and uses it to drive the catastrophe geometry you have been feeling. When the Prolog system hits crisis, the disc snaps. When it finds a new strategy, the tension relaxes. You are watching the same thing from two sides.

The bridge visualizes the ORR cycle (Observe-React-Reorganize) in real time. Tension accumulates during computation (solve/6 tracking inference cost). When cost exceeds the threshold, crisis fires. The execution handler classifies the crisis and initiates recovery. Each event — computation_start, crisis_detected, oracle_consulted, synthesis_attempted — appears in the timeline.

This bridge is doing what the Lakos Project did in 1986: modeling individual children's mathematical knowledge as logical propositions in a Prolog machine, then making the internal state visible. The Lakos Project modeled memory nodes, misconceptions, and cognitive conflicts. This bridge adds real-time tension dynamics and catastrophe geometry to the same basic idea.

Together they show the same phenomenon from three representations: continuous (tension), geometric (disc), and discrete (events). Borasi, R. (1994). Capitalizing on errors as "springboards for inquiry." JRME, 25(2), 166-208. Drawing on Lakatos, I. (1976), Proofs and Refutations, Cambridge University Press. Borasi demonstrates that mathematical knowledge historically grows through a dialectic of proofs and refutations. Errors are not obstacles but generative: "the doubt and cognitive dissonance generated by an error can force students to rethink the foundations of their mathematical definitions." The bridge makes this dialectic visible — crisis is not the system failing but the system generating the conditions for its own reorganization.

Tension & Stability

That was a crisis. The learner's counting strategy could not finish within the inference limit. The tension spiked, stability went negative, and the system called for help.

In the Prolog formalization, "help" means the teacher intervened — not by handing over a strategy, but by creating conditions where the learner's current approach could no longer hold. What the system receives is a result and a description, not the internal workings. The snap on the machine corresponds to this moment — the disc's state changed, but the springs don't remember being stretched.

The gold dots on the tension chart mark where tension relaxed after a crisis. Each one is a snap.

Run a few more computations. Each crisis adds to the record below. The tape logs every snap in sequence. The matrix remembers them structurally: each new snap negates the diagonal of the previous matrix — the same operation Cantor used in 1891 to prove that any enumeration of binary strings misses at least one. Here, each snap produces a state that was provably outside the previous totality of states.

The developmental story runs left to right across the preset buttons. A kindergartener counts by ones. A first-grader hits the limit of counting and learns to count on from the larger number. A second-grader needs place-value decomposition. A third-grader encounters an operation the system has never seen. Each transition is a crisis that demands a qualitatively different strategy — not a smoother version of the old one.

The Memory

The Tape

By now you have seen tension build and release several times. The shape repeats: accumulation, acceleration, crisis, partial relaxation. Partial — because the system does not return to zero after a snap. Tension carries forward. The learner's next computation starts harder than the last one started. This is hysteresis in the formal model, though the Zeeman machine's springs are ideal and forget their strain. The tape remembers what the physics cannot.

The formalization tracks this process at two levels that must not be confused. The meta-interpreter (solve/6) attempts computation — it is the "doing" layer. The incompatibility semantics module (proves/4) tracks inferential commitments — it is the "justifying" layer. A learner can do what it cannot justify, and can justify what it cannot do. The gap between these two is where the interesting things happen.

Fractions — What Works at the Action Level, What Does Not Yet Work at the ORR Level

The system handles whole-number arithmetic through the ORR cycle (Observe, React, Reorganize). For fractions, the action-level picture is concrete and the cycle-level picture is still open. Both deserve equal specificity:

What works at the action level. The registry holds fraction:area_model_part_of_part: a productive action automaton that performs fraction multiplication via the part-of-part area model. Alongside it sits a productive/deformation pair, cross_multiplication_rule_from_pattern and cross_multiplication_rule_without_ground. Both produce the same numeric result for A/B times C/D; the productive variant ends its trace with justify_via_area_model_part_of_part and carries an area_model justification; the deformation ends with skip_area_model_justification, carries no justification, and the deontic scorekeeper marks it commitment_without_entitlement(area_model_justification_missing). This is Van de Walle's "rules without reason" rendered structurally — same answer, different inferential standing.
What does not yet work at the ORR level. The meta-interpreter does not yet route fraction outcomes through the ORR cycle as recollection-compatible values, so a fraction action's result cannot drive a crisis the way a counting overflow can. Three-level unit coordination (Hackenberg) as a general ORR-negotiable scheme is not implemented. Metamorphic accommodation (Norton/Steffe: strategies synthesised by recursive composition of others) is not in the synthesis path.
Where the metaphors are known to break. The registry exposes the breaks rather than hiding them: metaphor_breaks_at/3 lists known break-points by metaphor (the Lakoff & Núñez "Arithmetic Is Object Collection" metaphor breaks at fractions, for example), and metaphor_repair/4 names the ad-hoc moves that re-cover the metaphor when it tears. The breaks are queryable features, not embarrassments.

The boundary the system now exhibits is not "cannot represent fractions" — it is "represents fraction multiplication at the action level, but does not yet negotiate fraction acquisition at the ORR-cycle level." The formalization is a tool for noticing both halves of that distinction.

Questions to sit with

What changes between the kindergarten run and the Grade 1 run? Not in the numbers — in the shape of the tension curve.
The teacher provides strategies but the learner does not build them. When does receiving a method become understanding it? The formalism cannot say.
Each snap on the matrix negates the previous diagonal. The new state is provably outside the old totality. But the matrix is finite and wraps at 8 rows. What gets lost when the memory is bounded?
The fraction boundary is where flat FSM synthesis fails. What kind of computation would be needed to cross it? The manuscript calls it "metamorphic accommodation." The code does not implement it.

What This Page Can and Cannot Show

It can show tension building and snapping — the shape of crisis as catastrophe geometry. It can show the tape accumulating, the matrix growing, the discontinuity between one strategy and the next.

It cannot show the felt quality of learning. Carspecken's "sound of time" — the pre-reflective ground of temporal experience — is not a tension curve. A resource limit is not the same as running out of breath. The numbers exhaust; the child tires. These are not the same kind of exhaustion.

It cannot show the teacher as a person. The teacher in the code creates conditions for crisis, but the interaction is one-directional. Carspecken's I-Thou involves mutual recognition — both parties are changed by the encounter. Nothing in the code models the teacher being changed. The blue ball pulls; it does not listen.

It cannot show the moment where formalism hands off to human judgment. The sequent calculus has a precise boundary — the arche-trace erasure — where formal derivations succeed structurally but the proof object carries no content. That boundary marks where the code honestly stops being able to say anything. This page can describe it; it cannot perform it.

The More Machine shows the embodied side of the metaphor — what it feels like to drag a system through crisis with your hand. This page shows the computational side — what the Prolog server records when crisis is driven by inference limits rather than spring tension. Neither is complete. The bridge between them is the bridge between formalization and experience, which is the manuscript's central concern.
