You have seen the nesting. Now feel it. Each node in these rings is itself a little catastrophe machine — it can snap, just like the More Machine on page 2. When you drag a node's control point, you are building tension in that one machine. But the machines are connected by springs. Tension propagates. One snap can trigger another.

And when enough snaps accumulate — crisis — a new ring appears. This is how a new strategy emerges: not by being told, but by accumulating enough tension that the old structure has to expand.

This couples the catastrophe physics with the strategy automata. Each node's snap represents a local perturbation (Steffe, 1992). The spring network models how perturbation in one part of a strategy propagates to connected parts. The fractal growth (new rings materializing from accumulated snaps) models the developmental sequence: enough local crises trigger global reorganization.

Two-layer physics: node-local Zeeman gradient descent (the catastrophe) and network-global Verlet spring integration (tension propagation). The separation is critical — the snap is sudden (gradient descent finds the other basin), the jiggle is smooth (Verlet with damping). This mirrors the distinction between determinate negation (the specific snap) and the continuous tension that precedes it.
