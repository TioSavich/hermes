# Does It Make Sense?
_Anchor ID: `IM-G2-U7-L17` — Grade grade2, Unit 7, Lesson 17_

## Learning Goals (teacher-facing)
- Critique (orally and in writing) estimates made when problem solving and explain how to improve an estimate to make it more reasonable.
- Explain (orally) estimation strategies for adding or subtracting within 1,000.

## Student-Facing Learning Goals
Let’s decide if our answers make sense.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to use strategies based on place value to decide if answers to story problems and the values they find when adding and subtracting make sense.

Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to use strategies based on place value to decide if answers to story problems
and the values they find when adding and subtracting make sense.


Narrative
This lesson is optional because it is an opportunity for additional practice with estimating and with addition and
subtraction within 1,000 that not all classes may need. Students also are invited to make sense of reasoning about a
story problem that involves addition within 1,000.

In previous lessons, students used estimation strategies to decide if answers to one- and two-step story problems made
sense. In this unit, students added and subtracted within 1,000, using a variety of different methods and
representations, with an emphasis on place value.

In this lesson, students continue to develop their estimation strategies when problem solving, and use estimation
strategies to determine whether their calculations make sense. As in previous lessons with estimation, students may use
any strategy that makes sense to them. Some students may continue to use simple front-end estimation, while others
may begin to consider how to make adjustments to their initial estimates, based on the values of the places. For
example, when estimating the value of               , some students may add the hundreds and estimate 700, while
others may start with 700 as the estimate and then adjust it to 800 because they can tell that        will be at least 100
more. Both estimates are acceptable for grade 2 students, although by the end of the lesson, they should be able to
explain why the latter estimate may be closer to the actual answer than the former.

Although the work of this lesson does encourage students to begin to consider precision in their estimates, it is
important to continue to emphasize the reasoning students use to make their estimates, rather than how close an
estimate is to an actual answer. Also, keep in mind that although students’ strategies may resemble “rounding,” they will
not round numbers formally until grade 3.
