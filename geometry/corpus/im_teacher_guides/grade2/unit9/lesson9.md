- Compare and contrast (orally) story problems.

Let’s analyze story problems.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to compare, connect, and solve story problems.

Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to compare, connect, and solve story problems.


Narrative
In previous lessons, students solved all the types of story problems including Add To, Take From, Put Together, and
Compare, with the unknown in all positions. In this lesson, students sort a variety of story problems, including two-step
problems, and describe the categories they create. They then choose to solve one of the story problems using a method
that makes sense to them. This lesson can be used to assess how students think about and make sense of story
problems and to identify the methods and representations students are most comfortable using to solve story problems
(MP2).

Students work with story problems within 100 to reinforce the fluency expectation for grade 2.

Card Sort: Story Problems

     Standards                                                     Instructional Routines
Addressing     2.OA.A.1                                         • Card Sort


The purpose of this activity is to sort story problems into categories students choose. Students may sort the problems
based on whether they are one-step problems or two-step problems. They may also use addition, subtraction, or both
addition and subtraction as their categories. This sorting process may create some ambiguity since students might sort
based on the operation suggested by the overall story structure or by the operation they would use to solve the
problem. Some of the two-step stories may require both operations to solve. If students use the operations to sort and
sort in different ways, this provides a good opportunity for discussion.

This sorting task gives students opportunities to analyze story problems closely and make connections (MP7). The goal
of the Activity Synthesis is to share the different characteristics students used to sort the problems.


     Access for English Language Learners
     MLR8 Discussion Supports. Invite students to begin partner interactions by repeating the question, “What
     categories did you choose?” or, “Why did you put this problem into this category?” This gives both students an
     opportunity to produce language.
     Advances: Conversing


     Access for Students with Disabilities
     Action and Expression: Internalize Executive Functions. Check for understanding by inviting students to rephrase
     directions in their own words. Focus the students’ thinking by asking, “What is the action in the problem? What is
     actually happening in the problem? How many steps would you need to solve?” Giving a little direction through
     questioning may help focus some students.
     Supports accessibility for: Memory, Organization, Attention
