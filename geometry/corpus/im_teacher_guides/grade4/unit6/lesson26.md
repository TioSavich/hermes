Narrative
This lesson is optional because it does not address any new mathematical content standards. This lesson does provide
students with an opportunity to apply precursor skills of mathematical modeling (MP4). In this lesson, students build on
their prior understanding and experiences with creating patterns to solve a multi-step problem in a real-world context.

In the first activity, students make different types of paper flowers. In the second activity, students develop a plan for
how many paper flower garlands their group could make in three hours. To develop their plan, students use information
given in the task, information from the previous activity, and make decisions about details not given in the task.

Paper Flower Construction

     Standards
Building Toward     4.MD.A.1, 4.MD.A.2, 4.OA.A.3


The purpose of this activity is for students to make paper flowers. Students use information they learn during this
experience to develop a plan for making paper flower garlands in the next activity. Specifically, they learn how many
sheets of tissue paper are used for each paper flower and how many paper flowers their group can make in 10 minutes.
For this reason, it is important that the time given for small-group work is kept to 10 minutes.

     ◦ 20 sheets cut into strips that are 4 inches by 9 inches.
     ◦ 40 sheets cut into strips that are 6 inches by 12 inches (length should be about 2 times the width).

Plan Quinceañera Decorations

     Standards
Building On     4.OA.C
Addressing      4.MD.A.1, 4.MD.A.2, 4.OA.A.3


The purpose of this activity is for students to create a plan for how many paper flower garlands their team could make
in 3 hours. Students use information given in the task and information they learned in the previous activity to develop
their plan. They also make decisions about information not given in the task, such as the size and colors of the paper
flowers, the pattern used in the garland, the size and spacing of the flowers on the garland, and the specifics of how to
use the three hours. When students identify information they need to know to answer a question and consider
assumptions about information not given in a situation, they model with mathematics (MP4).

Although students are using what they learned in the previous activity to estimate how many flowers they can make in 3
hours, avoid using or encouraging more formal rate language (for example, 12 flowers per 10 minutes or 72 flowers per
hour). Encourage students to use what they know about equal groups to estimate how many flowers they can make (for
example, for each group of 10 minutes, I can make 14 flowers) and encourage them to adjust their estimates based on
other factors (for example, planning for extra time to attach the flowers to the string). Students will learn more about
rates and solving rate problems in grade 6.

Lesson Synthesis
“Today, we created a plan for how many paper flower garlands a team could create in three hours.”

“Some information was given to you to create a plan. How did you make decisions about the information you weren’t
given?” (For our pattern, we just thought 1 small flower, then 2 large flowers would look nice. For the amount of flowers
we could make, we made an estimate based on how many flowers our team made in 10 minutes. We also decided to
have 1 member of our team attach the flowers to the garland. We decided on a length of 20 feet for each garland
because we thought that would go across the whole side of a room.)

Consider having students respond to the previous question as a journal prompt.
