# Multiply Two-Digit Numbers and One-Digit Numbers
_Anchor ID: `IM-G4-U6-L6` — Grade grade4, Unit 6, Lesson 6_

## Learning Goals (teacher-facing)
- Comprehend (in spoken language) the meaning of the term “partial products.”
- Interpret (orally and in writing) diagrams that represent partial products as a strategy for multiplying two-digit and one-digit whole numbers.
- Represent products of two-digit and one-digit numbers with a diagram and calculate the value.

## Student-Facing Learning Goals
Let’s multiply two-digit and one-digit numbers.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to multiply a two-digit number and a one-digit number using place value understanding.

The purpose of this lesson is for students to multiply a two-digit number and a one-digit number using place value

Narrative
In the previous lesson, students solved two-digit multiplication problems in a way that made sense to them. Students
discussed decomposing factors and considered different representations of their strategy. In this lesson, students
extend these ideas to find the value of products beyond 100, focusing on representations and strategies based on place
value and the properties of operations, which are familiar from IM Grade 3.

Students analyze base-ten diagrams and diagrams that involve rectangles, some of which are partitioned by place value.
Students explain how the diagrams represent multiplication and make connections between them, deepening their
understanding of place value and properties of operations. At the end of the lesson, students consider a rectangular
diagram that will be used through the rest of the section.

Activity 2                                                                                                     15 min

Two Kinds of Diagrams

      Standards
Addressing     4.NBT.B.5


This activity continues to encourage place value reasoning for finding the product of a two-digit factor and a one-digit
factor.

Students make sense of two representations that show the two-digit factor decomposed by place value: a base-ten
diagram and a rectangle. The latter looks like an area diagram that students have used in IM Grade 3, where the side
lengths of a rectangle represent two factors. As the factors become larger, however, it becomes necessary to draw
rectangles whose side lengths are not proportional. When rectangles no longer accurately represent area, the term
“area diagrams” is not used. Instead, “rectangular diagrams” is used in teacher materials and “diagrams” in student
materials.

Students then choose a representation to use to find products and write corresponding expressions. In the Activity
Synthesis, they learn that the results of multiplying a part of one factor by the other factor can be called “partial
products.” In future lessons, students will use rectangular diagrams to represent multiplication of larger numbers.
