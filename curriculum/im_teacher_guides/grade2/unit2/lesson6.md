# Compare Methods for Subtraction
_Anchor ID: `IM-G2-U2-L6` — Grade grade2, Unit 2, Lesson 6_

## Learning Goals (teacher-facing)
- Compare and contrast (orally) strategies and recording methods for subtracting a one-digit number from a two-digit number.
- Describe (orally) how a base-ten diagram and equations represent the same subtraction strategy.

## Student-Facing Learning Goals
Let’s compare ways to subtract.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to compare methods for subtracting a one-digit number from a two-digit number with and without decomposing a ten.

The purpose of this lesson is for students to compare methods for subtracting a one-digit number from a two-digit

Narrative
In the first activity, students consider 3 methods for finding the difference represented using base-ten diagrams. In the
second activity, students find the difference with and without decomposing a ten and represent their thinking using
base-ten diagrams, words, or equations. Students are not expected to draw their work with base-ten diagrams in a
specific way. Students should have access to base-ten blocks throughout the lesson and the Cool-down. Students
compare their methods, and the teacher records student thinking using base-ten diagrams and equations in the Activity
Synthesis. In the Lesson Synthesis, students consider different ways to represent decomposing.

Introduce Target Numbers—Subtract Tens or Ones

     Standards
Addressing     2.NBT.B.5


The purpose of this activity is for students to subtract numbers within 100 with and without decomposing a ten.
Students learn Stage 4 of the Target Numbers center, which was introduced in IM Grade 1. For the introduction of this
stage, have students start at 99 and subtract tens or ones to get as close to 0 as possible. Students start by representing
99 with base-ten blocks and then take turns flipping a number card and choosing whether to subtract that number of
tens or ones and write an equation. The value of the difference becomes the first number in the next equation. The
player who gets closest to 0 in 6 rounds, without going below 0, is the winner.
