# A Change Unknown
_Anchor ID: `IM-G1-U2-L4` — Grade grade1, Unit 2, Lesson 4_

## Learning Goals (teacher-facing)
- Comprehend the notation “       ” can be used to represent an unknown in an equation.
- Explain (orally) strategies for finding the value of an unknown addend, including counting on.

## Student-Facing Learning Goals
Let’s solve story problems and compare ways to find an unknown addend.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to interpret a strategy for solving an Add To, Change Unknown problem that involves using drawings to count on. Students use strategies that make sense to them to determine the number that makes an equation true.

Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to interpret a strategy for solving an Add To, Change Unknown problem that
involves using drawings to count on. Students use strategies that make sense to them to determine the number that
makes an equation true.


Narrative
In previous lessons, students represented Add To story problems including problems where the change was unknown.
Students compared different ways to determine an unknown, including counting on. In this lesson, students make sense
of a way to represent counting on from one number to another in the context of a Add To, Change Unknown story
problem. Students are then invited to use strategies that make sense to them to determine the number that makes an
equation true.

In this lesson, students revisit the meaning of an equation, which is first introduced in IM Kindergarten as a way to
represent story problems when all quantities are known. Students are not expected to write equations on their own
until Section D of this unit, but students may begin to try writing equations on their own before then.

Practice Making Equations True

     Standards
Addressing     1.OA.C.5, 1.OA.C.6, 1.OA.D.7, 1.OA.D.8


The purpose of this activity is for students to continue to make sense of equations with a symbol for an unknown
addend and to compare strategies for determining the number that makes an equation true. Students continue to share
and compare addition strategies, including those based on counting on.

Although it is recommended to focus on counting on strategies in the Lesson Synthesis, monitor for other strategies
students may use. For example, some students may represent the total and separate the known addend, count back
from the total to the known addend, or use a known fact.


     Access for English Language Learners

     makes an equation true. As students share their strategies, annotate the display to illustrate connections. For
     example, next to each representation of a strategy, write labels to represent each number in the equation,
     including the unknown.
