Two ways to say no

A fourth grader adds two fractions and writes 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5. A teacher has two kinds of no available.

The first kind says: that's not right. It marks an absence — the correct answer did not appear. It gives the student nothing to stand on and the teacher nothing to work with.

The second kind says: you added the tops and added the bottoms — and there are places in mathematics where exactly that move is correct. Combining game records works this way: 2 hits in 5 at-bats plus 1 hit in 3 at-bats really is 3 hits in 8 at-bats. The move has a home. What it collides with is what fraction addition demands: a common unit before any counting starts. You cannot add halves to thirds any more than you can add apples to weekdays.

The second no has parts: the rule the student is actually following, the place where that rule works, and the specific commitment, a move made without earning the right to it (Brandom, Between Saying and Doing), that it collides with here. Researchers have been documenting errors at this level of detail for four decades. This system encodes that structure directly:

1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5
rule followedadd numerators and add denominators, componentwise
where it is validratio combination — pooling at-bats, mixing paint, merging tallies
what it collides withfraction addition requires a common unit before adding numerators

Why the engine has no built-in "not"

The reasoning engine under this site is written in Prolog, a language with no native logical negation. Its only built-in refusal means "no derivation found" — absence again, the first kind of no. We treat that limit as the design. Because the language supplies no not, every no the system can say had to be written down as content: a rule, its home domain, and the commitment it collides with, each tied to the study that documented it. The second kind of no — the kind with parts — is the only kind the system has.

The philosophical wager (after Hegel, by way of Robert Brandom): this is the right order of explanation. Incompatibility between commitments comes first; the tidy logical operator is an abbreviation for it. A no with parts can be repaired, and repair — not correctness — is where mathematical understanding grows.
