When two definitions live at once. Geometry stops being one voice. Two trapezoid definitions live in the curriculum, and the choice between them shapes the whole hierarchy that follows. You will have to choose, and stand behind the choice.

Materials

  • Who Is This Trapezoid Anyway

The article gives two live definitions of trapezoid. One says a trapezoid has at least one pair of parallel sides. The other says it has exactly one pair of parallel sides. Both appear in curriculum materials, and each produces a different classification story.

This prompt assumes that a teacher should not hide the conflict. You may disagree, but you must say what hiding it would protect.

Purpose

To practice choosing, explaining, and stress-testing a definition when more than one convention exists.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, make a definition-choice artifact.

  1. State the trapezoid definition you would use in this course or in an elementary classroom: at least one pair of parallel sides, exactly one pair of parallel sides, or another carefully worded version.
  2. Give one test case your definition includes.
  3. Give one test case your definition excludes.
  4. Say what happens to parallelograms under your definition.
  5. Write one sentence you would say to a student who learned the other definition.
  6. Name one risk in your choice: confusion, hierarchy pressure, conflict with a textbook, too much formality, or something else.

End with this sentence:

I would stand behind this definition when…

Aim for 250-350 words.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to a classmate who chose a different definition or named a different risk.

Use your classmate’s definition. In your reply:

  1. Name one classification consequence of their definition.

  2. Name one classroom advantage of their choice.

  3. Name one student confusion their choice might create.

  4. Offer one test question or diagram that would help decide whether the definition is working.

  5. Add one sentence beginning:

    Your definition would change my hierarchy diagram by…

Return Post

Return to your definition-choice artifact. Revise the definition, defend it, or name the convention note you would give students.

End with this sentence:

The definition became more usable when…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Requirements

Submit your initial post, your peer uptake reply, and your return post by the dates listed in Canvas.
