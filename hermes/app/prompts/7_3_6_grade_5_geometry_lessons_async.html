Making structure public. Grade 5 hides structure inside reasonable-looking answers. The teacher's move is to bring the structure out — to make it sayable, defensible, and shared among children.

The two Grade 5 lessons ask students to coordinate examples with structure.

  • In Strategies for Finding Volume, children use drawings and descriptions of boxes to reason about rectangular prisms.
  • In Properties of Quadrilaterals, children classify quadrilaterals and discuss relationships among categories.

Both lessons can collapse into answer-getting unless the teacher presses for the structure behind the answer.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, choose one lesson.

Make a structure-press artifact:

  1. Name the lesson.
  2. Write one child strategy or claim from the lesson.
  3. Say what the strategy gets right.
  4. Say what the strategy might not yet justify.
  5. Write one teacher question that presses for structure without taking over.
  6. Name the structure: layers, unit cubes, dimensions, drawing-to-prism correspondence, attributes, subset relation, or quadrilateral hierarchy.

End with this sentence:

The structure I would press for is…

Aim for 250-350 words.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to someone who chose the other lesson.

In your reply:

  1. Name one part of their teacher question you would keep.

  2. Name one part you would refuse or sharpen.

  3. Translate their structure-press into the other lesson.

  4. Add one sentence beginning:

    These two lessons are similar because…

Return Post

Return to your structure-press artifact. Revise the teacher question, defend it, or name student evidence that would show the structure became public.

End with this sentence:

The teacher move worked if the child could…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Where this points: What changes, what stays. Structure made public is the precondition for the next question — once the mathematics can be said, the next prompt confronts identity itself: a shape moves, and we have to say what survives the motion.
