First station — Folding. Geometry begins in the hand: two edges meet, and we have to decide whether their meeting counts. The whole course keeps asking a version of this question. Today it wears its simplest form.

Materials

  • table of quadrilateral properties

A fold can make a property evident, but an evident fold is not yet a proof. A fold might show parallel sides, symmetry, congruent parts, or only a fact about one paper cutout.

For this discussion, use one folding test from Activity 2.5-1. Your job is to decide what the fold earns and what it does not earn yet.

Purpose

To practice using a physical action as mathematical evidence, then deciding whether that evidence is strong enough to add a property to the quadrilateral-properties table.

Try First

Try one of the folding tests for a kite, trapezoid, parallelogram, or rhombus. You may trace the shapes on paper and work with paper cutouts. Keep your fold marks visible.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, make a fold-evidence artifact.

  1. Name the shape and the folding-test question you chose.
  2. Describe exactly what you folded. Say what points, sides, angles, or diagonals lined up.
  3. State one property you think the fold supports.
  4. Say whether the evidence is about sides, angles, diagonals, symmetry, or parallel lines.
  5. Name one property the fold does not settle yet.
  6. Write one teacher sentence you could say to a child who says, “I can see it, so it must be true.”

End with this sentence:

The fold counts as evidence only if…

Aim for 200-300 words. A photo or sketch is welcome, but your explanation has to name the property the fold is testing.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to a classmate who tested a different shape.

Use your classmate’s fold as evidence. In your reply:

  1. Name one claim from their post you would be willing to inherit into your own property table.

  2. Name one hidden assumption or missing test in their fold.

  3. Suggest one extra fold, counterexample, or special case that would test the claim.

  4. Add one sentence beginning:

    I would not add this property to the table yet if…

Return Post

Return to your first post. Revise one property claim, defend it with a clearer reason, or name the exact test still needed.

End with this sentence:

My folding evidence became more mathematical when…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Requirements

Submit your initial post, your peer uptake reply, and your return post by the dates listed in Canvas.

Where this points: The move into language. What you decide here about folds you will be asked to decide again about words — and a word that helps a child today can sediment into a false rule by next year.
