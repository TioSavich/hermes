What the teacher refuses. Grade 2 sharpens the question of teacher voice. A refusal can be as much a teaching move as a question — and refusing well is one of the hardest crafts in the room.

The two Grade 2 lessons put pressure on different teacher moves.

  • In Sorting Quadrilaterals, children sort shapes using four sides and right angles.
  • In Describing and Building Rectangles, children build rectangles with tiles, then describe and draw what they built.

This discussion asks what a teacher should say no to, while still preserving the child’s geometric work.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, choose one lesson and make a teaching decision artifact.

  1. Name the lesson you chose.
  2. Name one child action from the lesson that you would protect.
  3. Write one child sentence that could happen in the lesson.
  4. Say what you would refuse in that sentence or action.
  5. Say what you would preserve.
  6. Write the next teacher question you would ask.

End with this sentence:

The boundary I would protect is…

Aim for 250-350 words.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to a classmate who chose the other lesson. If none is available, reply to a classmate with no substantive reply yet.

In your reply:

  1. Name one part of their teacher question that would help a child.

  2. Name one part you would revise because it tells too much, asks too little, or hides the geometry.

  3. Translate their move into the other lesson.

  4. Add one sentence beginning:

    Your lesson makes me rethink…

Return Post

Return to your initial teacher question. Revise it, defend it, or name the child evidence that would decide whether it worked.

End with this sentence:

The teacher helps without taking over when…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Where this points: Where definitions meet. Single shapes will soon stop being enough — the arrows between categories begin to carry the mathematics, and the next prompt asks whether the hierarchy you draw can survive its own definitions.
