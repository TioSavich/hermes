What a teacher protects. The long climb begins for children in Grade 1. What a future teacher refuses to give away here decides what is possible later — the teacher move is the geometry.

The two Grade 1 lessons do different kinds of geometry work.

  • In Three Ways to Fill an Outline, children compose and decompose shapes.
  • In Describing Quadrilaterals, children select, sort, describe, and compare shapes.

This discussion asks you to decide what a teacher should protect in each lesson. You may also disagree with the prompt’s comparison if you think the two lessons are doing the same kind of work.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, choose one of the two lessons.

Make a teaching decision artifact with five parts:

  1. The lesson you chose.
  2. One child action from the lesson that you would want to preserve.
  3. One teacher move you would refuse because it would make the geometry too narrow, too easy, too verbal, too visual, or too teacher-controlled.
  4. One geometry idea from the Grade 1 Progressions that the lesson can develop.
  5. One sentence you could say to a child that presses for reasoning without simply saying “right” or “wrong.”

Your post should include one concrete detail from the lesson: a pattern-block outline, fewest/greatest number of blocks, quadrilaterals in a set of shapes, drawing triangles and quadrilaterals, sorting, sides, corners, or comparing shapes.

End with this sentence:

The teacher move I would protect is…

Aim for 250-350 words.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to a classmate who chose the other lesson. If you cannot find one, reply to a classmate who has no substantive reply yet.

In your reply:

  1. Name one part of their teacher move that you would keep.

  2. Name one part you would push back on. Be specific: too much telling, too little structure, not enough attention to attributes, not enough room for child strategy, or not enough connection to the Progressions.

  3. Translate their teacher move into the other lesson. For example, if they chose filling an outline, say what a similar move would look like in quadrilateral sorting.

  4. Add one sentence beginning:

    Your lesson choice makes me notice…

Return Post

Return to your initial teaching decision.

Write one paragraph that does exactly one of these:

  • changes the teacher move you would protect;
  • keeps the move but sharpens what it is allowed to do;
  • says what student evidence would show that your move worked.

Then finish:

The difference between helping and taking over is…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Where this points: When two definitions live at once. The grades will get older, and the questions will lose their single answer — the next prompt confronts a curriculum that quietly offers two trapezoid definitions and asks you to choose.
