Where definitions meet. Single shapes are no longer enough. The arrows between categories now carry the mathematics, and inclusive definitions either hold the hierarchy together or break it. The diagram is itself an argument.

Materials

Inclusive definitions make some quadrilaterals belong to more than one category. A hierarchy diagram is useful only if its arrows preserve the definitions we agreed to use.

This prompt assumes inclusive definitions are worth protecting. You may disagree, but you must say what the exclusive definition would help a learner see or avoid.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, choose one arrow or missing arrow from one hierarchy diagram.

Make a hierarchy-test artifact:

  1. Name the two categories connected by the arrow, or the two categories you think should be connected.
  2. State the definition that matters for each category.
  3. Give one example that belongs in the smaller category.
  4. Explain why that example must also belong in the larger category.
  5. Name one possible counterexample or pressure case: square, rectangle, rhombus, kite, trapezoid, isosceles trapezoid, or slanted parallelogram.

End with this sentence:

I would trust this arrow only if…

Aim for 250-350 words.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to a classmate who used a different arrow or diagram.

In your reply:

  1. Say what part of their arrow test you accept.

  2. Say what part you would still verify or refuse.

  3. Offer one pressure case that could strengthen or break the arrow.

  4. Add one sentence beginning:

    This hierarchy would mislead a learner if…

Return Post

Return to your arrow. Revise it, defend it, or name the definition or counterexample still needed.

End with this sentence:

The hierarchy became more mathematical when…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Where this points: Adding pressure without taking over. Hierarchy gave the mathematics its shape; the next prompt returns to teacher craft — Grade 3 — with a sharper instrument: how to press on a child's reasoning without lifting it out of their hands.
