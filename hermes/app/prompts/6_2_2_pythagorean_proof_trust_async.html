Showing is not proving. Animations rearrange area beautifully. The deepest form of the first folding question returns: a convincing motion is not yet a proof, until you can say what the rearrangement earned and what it left for someone else to finish.

The interactive Pythagorean theorem proofs all point toward a^2 + b^2 = c^2, but they do not earn trust in the same way. Some rely on rearranging area. Some rely on congruent pieces. Some make a relationship evident but still need an explanation.

This prompt assumes a proof should do more than show a convincing animation. You may disagree, but you must say what the animation itself earns.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, choose Method 5, 6, or 7.

Make a proof-trust artifact:

  1. Name the method you chose.
  2. Describe the key movement, rearrangement, or area comparison in your own words.
  3. Name one thing the figure makes visible.
  4. Name one thing the figure has not earned yet.
  5. Write the sentence you would use to explain why the method proves a^2 + b^2 = c^2.
  6. Label your explanation: stand behind, borrowing, or need to earn.

End with this sentence:

I would trust this proof only if…

Aim for 250-350 words.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to a classmate who chose a different method.

Do not grade the proof. Use it.

In your reply:

  1. Name one step in their explanation you would be willing to inherit.

  2. Name one step you would verify before passing it on.

  3. Say what would make that step more trustworthy: congruent pieces, equal area, preserved side length, a square argument, or a diagram label.

  4. Add one sentence beginning:

    The animation is not enough if…

Return Post

Return to your proof-trust artifact. Revise one step, defend one step, or name the exact support still needed.

End with this sentence:

The proof became more than a picture when…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Where this points: Looking at how children look. Proofs taught us how trust is built; the next prompt steps back from the mathematics itself and watches the watcher — Van Hiele levels make us observers of how children come to objects of thought.
