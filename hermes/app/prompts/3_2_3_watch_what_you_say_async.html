The move into language. Folds did real work. Now the teacher's words have to carry some of what paper carried before, and the question of what counts as evidence migrates into a sentence said out loud.

Materials

  • Watch What you Say

The article asks teachers to be careful with the words they use for shapes. A phrase that helps a young child today can become a false rule later. A phrase that is mathematically precise can also be too compressed for the child in front of you.

For this discussion, choose one classroom moment from the reading and write the teacher sentence you would actually say.

Purpose

To practice using teacher language that preserves a child’s current thinking while also moving toward properties, definitions, and relationships among shapes.

Quick Briefing

Use these ideas as you read and post:

  • The Van Hiele model is about what children can use as objects of thought: whole shapes, properties, relations among properties, and eventually deductive systems.
  • The “describe a quadrilateral over the phone” activity matters because it makes visual recognition insufficient. The listener needs properties.
  • Ricky’s “my teacher lied to me” moment is not just about blame. It is about how simplified definitions can help at one stage and obstruct at another.
  • Inclusive definitions create hierarchy. A square can be a rectangle when the definition of rectangle is property-based rather than prototype-based.

Initial Post

Before reading classmates’ posts, make a teacher-language artifact.

Choose one of these moments:

  • a child describing a quadrilateral over the phone;
  • Ricky asking whether an earlier teacher lied;
  • a child using an exclusive definition such as “a rectangle has two long sides and two short sides”;
  • one sample activity from the end of the article.

Then include all five parts:

  1. Write the exact sentence you would say to the child or class.
  2. Name the geometry idea your sentence is trying to protect.
  3. Name one word or phrase in your sentence that could mislead students later.
  4. Revise that word or phrase, or explain why you would keep it for this moment.
  5. Name the Van Hiele move you think the sentence supports: from whole shape to property, from property to relationship, or from relationship to explanation.

End with this sentence:

The word or phrase I would watch most carefully is…

Aim for 250-350 words.

Peer Uptake Reply

Reply to your assigned response partner. If no partner is assigned, reply first to a classmate who chose a different moment.

Use your classmate’s teacher sentence. In your reply:

  1. Name one part of the sentence that preserves the child’s thinking.
  2. Name one part that might flatten, overcorrect, or hide the mathematics.
  3. Offer a revised sentence that keeps something from their wording but changes one phrase.
  4. Add one talk move, such as “So are you saying…?”, “How could we check?”, or “Can someone add on?”

Return Post

Return to your original teacher sentence. Revise it, defend it, or name the child response that would make you change it.

End with this sentence:

My sentence became more teachable when…

If no one has replied to you by the return-post window, use the reply you wrote to a classmate as your outside pressure and say so.

Requirements

Submit your initial post, your peer uptake reply, and your return post by the dates listed in Canvas.

Where this points: What a teacher protects. From here the work is no longer abstract — it lives in a Grade 1 classroom, where teacher moves stop being theory and become decisions made in real time.
