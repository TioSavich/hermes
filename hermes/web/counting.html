Someone asks you: what is 7 + 5?

You count out seven marks. Then you count five more. Then you count them all from the beginning. One, two, three... twelve.

The counting-all strategy is the developmental ground floor. Every child begins here. The strategy is correct — it produces the right answer for any whole-number addition — but its cost is O(A + B), linear in the sum of the addends.

Steffe, L. P. (1992). Schemes of action and operation involving composite units. Learning and Individual Differences, 4(3), 259-309. Counting-all corresponds to Steffe's initial number sequence (INS): the child treats each unit as a perceptual item that must be individually produced. The transition to counting-on requires constructing the tacitly nested number sequence (TNS), where the first addend is held as a cardinal value.

The pedagogical question: at what problem size does counting-all become untenable? The answer isn't a fixed number — it depends on the child's working memory, patience, and context. But the pressure is always the same: the strategy costs too much for the problem at hand.

The counting PDA is the system's primordial stratum — the ground from which all subsequent strategies are elaborated. It is correct (terminates with the right answer for any finite input) but costly (O(A+B) inference steps).
