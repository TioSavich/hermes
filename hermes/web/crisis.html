A crisis doesn't tell you what to do next. It tells you that what you're doing now isn't enough. The specific way it's not enough determines what kind of help you need.

Steffe (1992) describes this moment as a perturbation — the child's current scheme is inadequate for the task. The perturbation is not a bug; it is the mechanism of development. Without it, there is no pressure to reorganize.

The necessity of disequilibrium is debated. Harel & Koichu (2010, JMB) define learning as a "continuum of disequilibrium-equilibrium phases." Simon (2013, JMB) challenges this directly: "disequilibrium is neither necessary nor sufficient to explain mathematics conceptual learning," citing students who construct new concepts through carefully sequenced tasks without obvious obstacles. The UMEDCTA model takes the Steffe/Harel position — crisis drives reorganization — but Simon's counterexamples mark where that assumption might be too strong.

The Prolog formalization classifies crises into four types:

efficiency_crisis — strategy works but costs too much

unknown_operation — operation type never encountered

normative_crisis — mathematical norms violated

incoherence_crisis — contradictory commitments, moves made without yet earning the right to them (Brandom, Between Saying and Doing)

What we just saw was an efficiency crisis. The strategy is correct but too expensive. The specific type of crisis determines what kind of new strategy can resolve it.

Brandom calls this the experience of error (Hegel's Erfahrung), a three-phase process: (1) Register — "the detection of an anomaly," acknowledging materially incompatible commitments; (2) Repair — "rectification by revising or rejecting some of the offending commitments"; (3) Recollect — "retrospective rational reconstruction that exhibits the course of experience as expressively progressive." The code implements Register and Repair. Recollection is absent — and that absence is where the formalization honestly stops.

The execution_handler.pl catches the perturbation and classifies it via classify_crisis/3, consulting the incompatibility semantics module. The crisis type determines the recovery strategy:

efficiency_crisis → find a strategy with lower strategy_runtime_cost/4

unknown_operation → consult oracle for new operation type

normative_crisis → validate against proves/4 sequent calculus

incoherence_crisis → retract contradictory commitments

This models the structure of what Hegel calls determinate negation: the specific failure determines the specific response. An efficiency crisis and an incoherence crisis don't just both "fail" — they fail differently, and the difference matters. The formalization captures the structural pattern, not the full philosophical concept.
