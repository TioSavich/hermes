# Solve It Your Way
_Anchor ID: `IM-G2-U2-L14` — Grade grade2, Unit 2, Lesson 14_

## Learning Goals (teacher-facing)
- Represent and solve one- and two-step Add To, Result Unknown problems.

## Student-Facing Learning Goals
Let’s solve story problems and share our thinking with others.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to represent and solve one- and two-step story problems. Students use representations to make sense of problems, support their calculations, and explain their thinking.

Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to represent and solve one- and two-step story problems. Students use
representations to make sense of problems, support their calculations, and explain their thinking.


Narrative
In previous lessons, students solved different problem types within 100. Students made sense of story problems and
connected story problems to diagrams and equations. The activities in this lesson can be used to assess the methods
students use to make sense of and solve story problems on their own. Students are introduced to the idea that story
problems could have more than one step.

In the first activity, students solve a Put Together/Take Apart, Total Unknown problem and then use their solution to
solve an Add to, Result Unknown problem. In the second activity, students work together to make sense of and solve a
two-step story problem where both parts are presented together.

This lesson has a Student Section Summary.

Make Sense of 2 Steps

     Standards                                                       Instructional Routines
Addressing     2.OA.A.1                                          • MLR6 Three Reads


The purpose of this activity is for students to make sense of and solve a two-step story problem using methods of their
choosing. Students reason about a two-step story problem that is presented as a single story. Look for the different
ways students identify what quantities they know and what they need to figure out. Look for the different ways students
represent the problem, including ways they may act out the problem with their partner. When students share, compare,
and ask questions about each other’s approaches for solving the problem, they construct viable arguments and critique
the reasoning of others (MP3).

Students begin the activity by looking at the problem displayed, rather than in their books. At the end of the Launch,
students open their books and work on the problem.

This activity uses MLR6 Three Reads. Advances: reading, listening, representing.
