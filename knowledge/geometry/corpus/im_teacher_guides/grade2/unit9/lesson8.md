# Add and Subtract within 100
_Anchor ID: `IM-G2-U9-L8` — Grade grade2, Unit 9, Lesson 8_

## Learning Goals (teacher-facing)
- Explain (orally) strategies for adding and subtracting within 100.

## Student-Facing Learning Goals
Let’s fluently add and subtract within 100.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to practice addition and subtraction within 100 to build fluency.

Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to practice addition and subtraction within 100 to build fluency.


Narrative
In previous lessons, students composed and decomposed units to add and subtract within 1,000. Students considered
which sums and differences within 100 were more or less difficult for them to find.

In this lesson, students develop fluency with adding and subtracting within 100. In the first activity, students play a new
version of Heads Up game to practice fluency. This version is structured differently than the game students played in
Section A. For example, students play in groups of 3 and use different recording directions. In the second activity,
students choose which center game from previous units to play based on what they know they need to practice.
Spinners and cards can be reused if they were saved from previous units or new cards can be printed for the centers
and stages recommended for this lesson.

The Cool-down should be completed before the Lesson Synthesis so that students can share their responses during the
Lesson Synthesis.

The purpose of this activity is to give students practice adding and subtracting within 100. Students work in groups of 3.
One student adds to find the sum of 2 numbers. The other students add or subtract to find the unknown addend when
one addend and the value of the sum are known. Although the majority of sums will fall within 100, some students may
create sums greater than 100.


      Access for English Language Learners
      MLR8 Discussion Supports. Synthesis: Display question starters to support small group discussion about their
      favorite role in the game: “Why did you . . . ?” and “Can you say more about . . . ?”
      Advances: Speaking, Conversing


      Access for Students with Disabilities
      Representation: Develop Language and Symbols. Support understanding of the problem by inviting students to
      act it out using manipulatives. For example, one student can build the numbers with base-ten blocks and the
      other students can complete the action of composing or decomposing to find and prove the answer.
      Supports accessibility for: Conceptual Processing, Attention
