# Ways to Represent Multiplication of Teen Numbers
_Anchor ID: `IM-G3-U4-L14` — Grade grade3, Unit 4, Lesson 14_

## Learning Goals (teacher-facing)
- Interpret (orally and in writing) diagrams and expressions that represent strategies for multiplication where one factor is a teen number.

## Student-Facing Learning Goals
Let’s make sense of some ways to represent the multiplication of teen numbers.

## Lesson Purpose
The purpose of this lesson is for students to make sense of representations of the multiplication of teen numbers.

A Factor Greater than 10

     Standards
Addressing          3.OA.B.5
Building Toward     3.OA.C.7


The purpose of this activity is for students to see how, when multiplying a number greater than 10, the distributive
property can be used to decompose the factor into tens and ones, creating two smaller products. Base-ten blocks and
base-ten diagrams are used to help students visualize what is happening when a factor is decomposed to make two
easily known products. Factors that are teen numbers can be naturally decomposed into a ten and some ones using
place value. This will be useful in subsequent lessons as students progress towards fluent multiplication and division
within 100.

When students see that you can decompose a teen number into tens and ones and use this to multiply teen numbers,
they look for and make use of structure (MP7).

Ways to Represent

     Standards
Addressing           3.OA.B.5
Building Toward      3.OA.C.7


The purpose of this activity is for students to make sense of different ways of representing multiplication of a teen
number. Students analyze a gridded area diagram, base-ten blocks, and an area diagram labeled with side lengths.
When they discuss how the different diagrams represent the same product, students reason abstractly and
quantitatively (MP2).
